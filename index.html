Research

Ocean mesoscale

Ocean mesoscale eddies are the “weather” of the ocean, with typical horizontal scales of less than 100 km and timescales on the order of a month.  Mesoscale eddies are ubiquitous in the ocean, and typically exhibit different properties to their surroundings, allowing them to transport properties such as heat, salt and carbon around the ocean. The water properties of eddies are also important in supplying nutrients to coastal zones and the surface ocean where plankton blooms may result. More than half of the kinetic energy of the ocean circulation is contained in the mesoscale eddy field.

Biophysical Interaction

The ocean food web is built on the productivity of tiny photosynthetic organisms called phytoplankton. Roughly 50% of global carbon fixation - the transformation of inorganic carbon (CO2), into organic carbon (sugars) - occurs in the ocean. The rate of phytoplankton productivity in the ocean depends on the amount of nutrients and solar energy available. Since nutrients are used up in the sunlit waters of the surface ocean as phytoplankton grow, the resupply of nutrients depends on physical oceanographic processes that carry or mix nutrient-rich waters upwards.

Weather in a tank Laboratory

In this laboratory, we simulate a rotating tank as our Earth model. A rotating tank with ice in the center can be modelled as two poles in the North and South and other higher or lower latitude zones. It helps us better understand our atmospheres, oceans, and climate. Laboratory experiments and demonstrations have made a considerable contribution to our understanding of fluid dynamics.
